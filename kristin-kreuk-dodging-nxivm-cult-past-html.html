Kristin Kreuk is dodging her NXIVM cult past

Publish date: 2024-02-10

Kristin Kreuk is back on TV as a crusading lawyer investigating town's dark secret, but you won't find her discussing any parallels to her own life.

The actress is firmly refusing to discuss her time as the top drawcard for Keith Raniere's creepy sex cult NXIVM, in which she spent seven years.

The 35-year-old sat down for numerous interviews about her new series Burden of Truth but none had even a single mention of her cult past.

Kristin Kreuk is firmly refusing to discuss her time as the top drawcard for creepy sex cult NXIVM in interviews for new TV show Burden of Truth (pictured at press event for the show)

Kreuk's publicists are even pre-screening reporters to make it clear the star would not be discussing that part of her life at all.

'We are not answering any questions about Allison Mack or NXIVM so I would need to make sure this is only about the series,' they told the Daily Beast.

She was allegedly the one who recruited Mack, who went on to become the queen bee of DOS, an all-woman clique of NXIVM that served as sex slaves for Raniere.

Both Raniere and Mack have been charged with a series of crimes over their involvement in the cult, which has only a handful of members left.

Kreuk, who was not charged with anything, denied any knowledge of the DOS side of the organization or that she lured anyone in.

She said she took a course at NXIVM when she was 23 that she understood to be a 'self-help/personal growth course that helped me with my previous shyness'.

Cult leader Keith Raniere has been charged with a series of crimes over his involvement in the cult, which has only a handful of members left

Kreuk (left) was allegedly the one who recruited Allison Mack (right), who went on to become the queen bee of DOS, an all-woman clique of NXIVM that served as sex slaves for Raniere

'I left about five years ago and have had minimal contact with those who were still involved,' she said.

'The accusations that I was in the 'inner circle' or recruiting women as 'sex slaves' are blatantly false. During my time, I never experienced an illegal or nefarious activity. 

'I am horrified and disgusted by what has come out about DOS. Thank you to all the brave women who have come forward to share their stories and expose DOS; I can't imagine how difficult this has been for you.

'I am deeply disturbed and embarrassed to have been associated with NXIVM. I hope that the investigation leads to justice for all of those affected.'

This statement has since appeared to be an attempt to draw a line under her involvement and Kreuk has consistently refused to discuss it further.

However, former NXIVM's former publicist Frank Parlato said Kreuk was definitely used as the cult's star recruitment tool during her membership.

Kruek (pictured in 2013 soon after leaving the cult) said she took a course at NXIVM when she was 23 that she understood to be a 'self-help/personal growth course that helped me with my previous shyness'

'She was a draw for those who wanted to meet her. She made herself available to recruit,' he said.

'I recall speaking with one radio show host who was eager to meet Kreuk and agreed to promote a NXIVM a-cappella event - if he could have Kreuk on the show. I set it up and he plugged the hell out of it, as I recall.'

The Daily Beast's reporter made the point that despite the embarrassment of her association with the cult, it could easily be used to promote her show. 

'Given the overlap between the real-life scandal Kreuk's become associated with and the central themes of her new series, those banned questions could have led to a fascinating conversation,' she wrote.
